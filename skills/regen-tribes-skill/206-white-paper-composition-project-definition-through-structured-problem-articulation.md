# White Paper Composition Project Definition Through Structured Problem Articulation

A white paper is a structured document that defines a project in terms that others can evaluate.

It is not marketing. It is not a feature list. It is a problem statement with a proposed resolution.

The purpose of the white paper is to determine whether the project is worth pursuing before resources are spent on it.

The term white paper originates from the concept of a clean document stripped of noise.

A clean document contains only what is necessary. Noise is anything that does not contribute to understanding.

The goal is to reach a reader who has limited time and must decide whether to engage.

Most project descriptions fail because they attempt to impress rather than inform.

Impressive language generates text that nobody reads. Informative language generates text that enables decisions.

The test of a good project description is simple: can a language model understand it?

If a language model cannot derive the next action from the description, the description is not clear.

Every creator faces the same challenge: they know their project intimately but cannot communicate it efficiently.

Every time the creator explains the project to a new person, they repeat the context-building exercise.

This repetition drains energy and introduces inconsistency. Different explanations produce different understandings.

The solution is to write the white paper once and use it as the canonical source of truth.

A well-constructed white paper filters out unnecessary projects before they consume resources.

If the creator cannot answer the white paper questions, the project is not ready.

If the creator can answer but the answers are vague, the project lacks foundation.

The white paper reveals gaps in thinking. Gaps are not failures. Gaps are information.

A project is defined by answers to these questions:

Question 1: what problem does this solve? The problem must be felt by a specific group of people in a specific situation.

Question 2: who experiences this problem? The audience must be identifiable. Generic audiences produce generic solutions.

Question 3: what does the audience do today to solve it? Understanding current behavior reveals where a new solution fits.

Question 4: what is the proposed resolution? The resolution is not the product. It is the change in the audience's situation.

Question 5: what does the audience gain from the resolution? Gains must be concrete and measurable.

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):

Question 6: what is the launch scenario? When the system first activates, what happens? Who acts first? What do they see?

Question 7: what is the minimum version that demonstrates the resolution? The minimum version tests the core assumption without building everything.

Question 8: how is success measured? If the resolution works, what changes? If it fails, what fails first?

The launch scenario is the main success scenario. It describes how the system activates for the first time.

The creator must specify: the first action taken, the first response from the system, the first outcome achieved.

A vague launch scenario indicates the creator has not resolved the core sequence of operations.

A precise launch scenario reveals the product structure. The sequence of operations becomes the product architecture.

A white paper is a plain document. Markdown, text, or any format that preserves structure.

Formatting is irrelevant. The questions must be answered. The answers must be specific.

Generalities do not enable decisions. Specificity enables action.

The creator must write from the position of the person receiving the document, not the person sending it.

The reader must be able to make a decision after reading. The decision is binary: engage or do not engage.

If the reader cannot decide, the document has failed.

Regenerative communities face the creator problem repeatedly. Each member who proposes an initiative must explain it.

The white paper process gives the community a standard format for project definition.

When a member proposes a project, they complete the white paper. The community evaluates the white paper.

This separates the idea from the person. Evaluation focuses on the document, not the reputation of the proposer.

Projects without clear problem statements are filtered out before consuming community resources.
