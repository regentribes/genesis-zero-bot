# Community Proof Of Concept Spiral Core Team Formation And Vision Alignment

The Proof-of-Concept Spiral validates that the vision is possible before committing resources.

It has two condition sets. Condition Set 1 focuses on core team and vision coherence. Condition Set 2 focuses on market and feasibility exploration.

The goal is assembling a committed core team with shared vision and evidence that the market and bioregion support the project.

The ideal founding team size is 5 to 7 people with a broader circle of 7 to 12.

Essential founder roles include the visionary dreamer who holds the why
and
inspiration, the operator pragmatist who makes things real, the financial strategist who understands economics, the design-minded person who thinks about how things fit,
and
the community heart person who focuses on relationships
and
culture.

Each founder completes an assessment covering commitment level, geographic location, available hours, top three skills to contribute, gaps for learning, and financial capacity.

Each founder is interviewed separately and together using structured questions.

The questions cover personal story on why drawn to regenerative community, the specific problem being solved, the wildest dreams vision for 10 years, non-negotiables that must be true to stay, fears
and
concerns about the project, risk tolerance,
and
timeline expectation.

These interviews reveal alignment and dealbreaker conflicts before deeper commitment.

The founding team produces a documented values statement with six components.

Component 1 is the regenerative commitment. Two to three sentences on what regeneration means to the group.

Component 2 is the community vision. What daily life feels like, who the community is, what is unique.

Component 3 is non-negotiables. Three to five things that cannot be compromised.

Component 4 is operating principles. How decisions are made, how members treat each other.

Component 5 is timeline expectation. Realistic acknowledgment of pace.

Component 6 is financial philosophy. What regeneration means economically.

The document is signed by all founders and referenced constantly throughout the project.

The team establishes decision-making and communication systems before proceeding.

This includes meeting cadence, format, decision protocol, documentation standards, communication channels, and conflict resolution procedures.

All decisions are recorded with date, who decided, what was decided, why, who dissented, and next review date.

The team defines the target community profile covering community type, ideal size range, member demographics, lifestyle sought, income level, geographic origin, commitment level, unique value proposition, and sustainability model.

The team conducts demand interviews with 20 to 30 people matching the ideal member profile.

The interview asks about current interest in community living, what would make community appealing, what would make someone say no, whether they would join if the community existed now, their timeline,
and
what they could contribute.

Market validation requires a minimum of 10 definitely interested people to proceed.

The team identifies 3 to 5 potential bioregions and researches each for climate, water availability, regulatory environment, land cost, and cultural alignment.

For each bioregion, the team assesses rainfall
and
growing season, water table
and
surface water, soil type
and
food production potential, solar
and
wind energy potential, zoning
and
environmental restrictions,
and
land costs per acre.

The team produces a preliminary financial model covering revenue streams, capital needs by category, funding sources, and basic operating budget.

This is rough at this stage. The purpose is testing whether the math works at all before investing in detailed planning.

The decision to proceed requires all of the following.

Three to 5 viable bioregions are identified.
Market validation shows demand from at least 10 definitely interested people.
Climate, water, and soil analysis is complete for each bioregion.
The regulatory landscape is mapped.
A rough financial model shows required capital.
A proof-of-concept design fits on typical land size.
The team understands major obstacles and confirms the vision is possible.
