# Asd Ste100-Simplified Technical English Controlled Language Documentation

ASD-STE100 Simplified Technical English is a controlled language standard for technical documentation.

It restricts the grammar and vocabulary available to writers to produce documentation readable by a global audience including non-native speakers.

The current version is Issue 9, released January 2025, with 53 writing rules and approximately 900 approved words.

Every concept is described by its first principles. No proprietary labels.

Technical documentation written in unrestricted English produces ambiguity.

The same sentence can be interpreted differently by readers from different linguistic backgrounds.

Complex sentence structures obscure meaning. Technical jargon excludes non-specialists.

Controlled language addresses these problems by restricting what writers can say and how they can say it.

The standard includes an approved dictionary of approximately 900 words.

Each word has a defined meaning. Only defined meanings may be used.

New words cannot be introduced without approval. If a word is not in the approved dictionary, it cannot be used.

This eliminates the problem of undefined technical terms creeping into documentation.

Sentence length is strictly limited.

Procedural sentences must not exceed 20 words. Descriptive sentences must not exceed 25 words.

Each sentence should convey only one instruction or one topic.

Paragraphs are limited to a maximum of six sentences.

Active voice is preferred. Passive voice should be avoided.

Simple verb tenses are used. Complex tense combinations are prohibited.

Articles must be used wherever grammatically required.

A and an are used for singular countable nouns.

The is used for specific items already identified.

Omitting articles where they are grammatically required is not permitted.

Noun clusters are sequences of nouns modifying another noun.

These must be limited to three words or fewer.

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):

Example: data transmission system is three nouns. Longer clusters must be broken into separate words or rephrased.

This prevents the dense noun stacks common in technical writing that obscure meaning.

Only approved words may be used. Technical terms must come from an approved technical dictionary.

Long compound words should be avoided.

Non-essential information must be eliminated.

One word should represent one specific function, procedure, or object.

Consistent language use is mandatory throughout all documentation.

Rule 1: write clear and concise sentences.

Rule 2: use approved words in approved meanings only.

Rule 3: use active voice.

Rule 4: use simple verb tenses.

Rule 5: keep sentences short.

Rule 6: keep paragraphs short.

Rule 7: use articles correctly.

Rule 8: limit noun clusters to three words.

Rule 9: do not use unnecessary words.

Rule 10: do not use non-approved words.

These rules are universal. All technical documentation following the standard must conform.

Type 1 is procedural content. This describes actions to be performed. It follows the 20-word limit.

Type 2 is descriptive content. This describes objects, states, or relationships. It follows the 25-word limit.

Mixing types within a single sentence is prohibited.

The standard originated in aerospace and defense documentation.

It is mandatory for NATO military equipment maintenance documentation.

It is widely used in aviation, space systems, and other safety-critical industries.

Any industry requiring unambiguous technical documentation can benefit from this approach.

The restrictions eliminate ambiguity by removing the sources of ambiguity.

Complex grammar creates misinterpretation. Short sentences limit misinterpretation.

Unrestricted vocabulary allows personal jargon. Approved vocabulary forces universal meaning.

Passive voice hides agency. Active voice forces clear attribution of who does what.

The notes in this repository follow principles similar to ASD-STE100.

They use short sentences with one idea per sentence.

They use active voice and present tense.

They avoid unnecessary filler words.

They define terms at first use and use them consistently.

The key difference: these notes use a broader vocabulary than the strict approved dictionary allows, but retain the structural discipline of the standard.

Regenerative community documentation benefits from simplified technical English principles.

Community governance documents should be readable by all members regardless of native language.

Procedure documents for water management or food processing should follow the 20-word procedural limit.

Descriptions of community roles and systems should follow the 25-word descriptive limit.

The approved word principle suggests maintaining a community glossary of defined terms used consistently across all documentation.
