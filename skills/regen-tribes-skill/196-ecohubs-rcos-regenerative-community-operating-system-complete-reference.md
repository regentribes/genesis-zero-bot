# Ecohubs Rcos Regenerative Community Operating System Complete Reference

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):

EcoHubs is an online community building the Regenerative Community Operating System.

The project creates an open-source blueprint for regenerative community design.

Stefan Lessle is the founder of EcoHubs and creator of RCOS.

RCOS provides a modular framework for explicit community structure.

The framework covers decision-making, roles, resource sharing, and conflict repair.

RCOS Core v0.1 was publicly released in January 2026.

EcoHubs envisions a growing network of small human-scale communities called EcoHubs.

EcoHubs regenerate land, culture, and livelihoods through cooperation and shared responsibility.

Each EcoHub is deeply adapted to local ecology and culture.

EcoHubs remain connected to a shared global commons for accelerated learning.

The network grows stronger as a distributed living system rather than a single solution.

An EcoHub is a small place-based community where people live, work, and learn together.

EcoHubs actively regenerate ecosystems they depend on.

They are intentionally human-scale: neither isolated communes nor rigid blueprints.

They are living systems shaped by local context, shared values, and real-world constraints.

Six principles define an EcoHub: regenerative living, shared infrastructure, community and care, local adaptation, learning by doing, and globally connected.

RCOS stands for Regenerative Community Operating System.

RCOS is an open-source shared system for organizing intentional communities.

It provides clear ready-to-use structures for governance so communities can focus on building.

RCOS makes community structure explicit so authority, responsibility, and repair do not drift into informal norms.

It is adaptable by design, grounded in practice, and focused on clarity over persuasion.

Communities fail for repeatable reasons: unclear membership, decision drift, resource ambiguity, and missing repair paths.

RCOS treats these as design problems: things that can be made explicit, tested, and improved.

Community living knowledge used to be local and implicit, passed through generations.

Today societies are more complex, interconnected, and regulated.

Without shared frameworks, new initiatives repeatedly fail on the same predictable issues.

Reinventing everything locally no longer works. Shared infrastructure for community knowledge is needed.

RCOS is a formal open standard.

RCOS is a layered operating system with clear responsibility boundaries across core layers.

RCOS is a shared language for governance where membership, economy, conflict, operations, and evolution are made legible.

RCOS is grounded in real failure modes with stress tests helping communities learn without collapsing.

RCOS is not software or a platform. It is a standard, not an app you install.

RCOS is not a DAO product. Governance tools can help but are not the system itself.

RCOS is not a fixed ideology. Structure comes before values; communities adapt without losing clarity.

RCOS is not a replacement for judgment. It reduces ambiguity but never replaces people.

Principle 1 is minimal at the core.
Start small and simple while remaining structurally sound as communities grow.

Principle 2 is explicit rather than implicit.
Make the invisible visible so assumptions do not go implicit.

Principle 3 is modular rather than monolithic.
Layers build on each other with clear boundaries.

Principle 4 is human scale rather than abstract.
Designed for groups of 5 to 150 people.

Principle 5 is failure aware rather than idealistic.
Built around constraints not ideals, assuming power exists, conflict arises, resources concentrate, informality favors some, and change happens.

RCOS is organized into layers with clear responsibility boundaries.

Nothing in higher layers can override the foundations.

Layer 0 is Purpose and Scope.
Defines the fundamental reason for the communitys existence and what it aims to govern.

Layer 1 is Membership.
Establishes explicit paths for joining and leaving the community with real exit paths.

Layer 2 is Governance.
Outlines how decisions are made from daily operations to constitutional changes.

Layer 3 is Economy and Resources.
Clarifies resource flows and mechanisms for protecting common assets from privatization.

Layer 4 is Conflict, Repair, and Accountability.
Establishes clear processes or a resolution ladder for addressing breakdowns in trust.

Layer 5 is Operations and Coordination.
Organ (metaphor for a functional subsystem or team):
Organizes daily tasks and responsibilities into defined roles to prevent burnout.

Layer 6 is Evolution and Adaptation.
Creates a framework for the community system to adapt and change over time.

Modules extend RCOS into real-world domains without changing core invariants.

Modules apply RCOS to specific domains like food systems, housing, education, or land stewardship.

Modules remain optional: communities choose what fits their context and constraints.

Modules stay compatible with the core: modules do not change core layer boundaries.

Modules are intentionally non-ideological: they describe structure and constraints, not the right way to live.

Modules are time-bounded, experimental, or removable.

If a module is not explicitly adopted, it does not apply.

Module 1 is Minimal Permaculture Module.
Designed for small or early-stage communities under twenty people with limited land.

Module 2 is Permaculture Module.
For communities where land stewardship is central to the project.

Additional modules listed: PermacultureEducation, Housing, Land Commons, Safeguards, Alternative Economies.

Clear invariants that must not be violated.

Explicit governance and decision logic.

Membership rules with real exit paths.

Economic and resource boundaries.

Conflict and accountability processes.

Change, versioning, and experimentation mechanisms.

RCOS includes stress tests based on real community failure modes.

These are not theoretical scenarios. They are based on failures observed again and again.

Examples include: dominant speakers controlling decisions, invisible power accumulation through responsibility, founder veto power, informal inner circles, burnout through invisible care labor, privatization of commons, emergency rule bypass,
and
conflict avoidance cultures.

Each stress test defines expected RCOS behavior, pass criteria, and fail criteria.

A system that only works in ideal conditions is not a real system.

Versioned on GitHub so changes are trackable and reviewable.

RCOS uses sociocracy as its governance model.

Consent replaces consensus to avoid paralysis.

Consent seeks no paramount objections making decisions good enough for now and safe enough to try.

This power-with model ensures efficiency without ignoring minority voices.

A Range of Tolerance means a proposal does not need to be perfect.

In sociocracy, an objection is treated as a gift: a vital piece of data indicating a proposal might interfere with the circle's aim.

A Paramount Objection must be a reasoned explanation of how a proposal creates risk, not personal preference.

Distributing authority into semi-autonomous circles can reduce change lag by up to 35 percent.

Double-linking connects circles: a Leader flows information down and a Delegate elected by the sub-circle flows information up.

Fruit Haven is an intentional community and ecovillage in Ecuador.

They worked with RCOS through guided facilitation sessions.

Results were immediate and concrete after only a few sessions.

The group began organizing ideas as distinct proposals, separating concerns, avoiding rabbit holes.

By revisiting old documentation they made roles, processes, and expectations explicit.

RCOS drew a precise line between the two and clarified where they overlap.

EcoTokens reward real-world regenerative work through contribution-based recognition.

Tokens are earned through contributions to shared knowledge and coordination.

Tokens unlock access, permissions, and influence within EcoHubs.

Tokens are non-transferable and non-speculative by design.

They exist to acknowledge effort, coordinate collaboration, and build trust.

Not designed for trading or accumulation for profit.

EcoHubs uses DAO-like governance emphasizing transparency and collective agency.

Snapshot is used for governance voting using token and NFT ownership.

Multi-signature treasury management protects against fraud.

Gnosis Safe multi-sig wallets require multiple trusted members to approve expenditures.

Blockchain provides immutable records, transparent transactions, smart contracts, and decentralized control.

The goal is not to replace human connection with code but to amplify trust and transparency.
