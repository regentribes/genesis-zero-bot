# Sociotechnical Management Governance Over Complex Collective Work

Sociotechnical management is the governance of complex collective work that requires both human collaboration and technical systems.

It applies to any endeavor that requires many interconnected workers producing results that feed into each other.

The source is 29 pages from a comprehensive Russian-language management guide by Alexander Pavlyut on apavlyut.ru.

Every concept is described by its first principles. No proprietary labels or vendor names.

Two systems of governance exist. They differ in where power resides.

Bureaucracy: power held by the office, by the position. The office holder issues instructions. Workers follow instructions to satisfy the boss.

Delocracy: power held by the work itself. The worker produces results that a consumer voluntarily pays for. The consumer is the boss.

In bureaucracy, the worker obeys the person above them. In delocracy, the worker obeys the outcome below them.

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):

The test of which system operates: where does the worker orient their effort? Toward the boss above or toward the consumer below?

Bureaucracy produces compliance. Delocracy produces value.

The Russian term for this guide: delocracy literally translates as power of the affair. English equivalents: workcracy or businesscracy.

A sociotechnical system is the combination of a production process and the labor relations that sustain it.

The system is the connection between components. A collection of isolated parts is not a system.

In a sociotechnical system, the unit of communication is always an official document. Any form of information must be representable as a document page.

The system always exists in all its states simultaneously: past, present, and future. The manager must hold all states in mind and in conversation.

The manager must unify not-yet-known interactions of people, data, documents, and equipment across all time frames.

An image of the future is a model of the sociotechnical system in its desired form.

It is not a vision statement. It is a concrete model that shows what the system looks like when it works.

The model enables: confident production of what is actually needed, consistent communication about plans, functional commissioning, and planned decommissioning.

The model shows the problem space, the existing solutions causing problems, who experiences those problems, and the proposed resolution within the products or services offered.

Without this model, teams produce what they think is needed, not what consumers actually need.

Target setting proceeds from the abstract to the concrete through three stages.

Stage 1: a goal is an abstract will to reach some unspecified point in the future.

Stage 2: a result is the concrete achievement of that point.
It is a thing formed in a specific place and time.
The goal and result form a dialectical pair, each generating the other.

Stage 3: a task is the assignment within the goal for a responsible party to achieve the result.

A task without a specified responsible party is not a task. It is a vague intention.

The goal must be connected to consumption. The goal is successful if the result is consumed one or more times by the intended user.

The primary goal metric is the engagement rate: how many times does the result get consumed, not how many results are produced.

A plan of work consists of two lists: the list of all results to be obtained, and the list of dependencies between results for the sequence of work.

The manager must be able to anticipate deviations from the plan and influence them. This requires high signal penetration through the organization.

Gantt charts become unreadable past fifty to one hundred items. They do not reliably show dependencies between work items.

A network graph shows both the list of work items and their sequential dependencies. It can remain legible at any scale.

The network graph identifies the critical path: the longest sequence of dependent work. Monitoring the critical path is the manager's primary responsibility.

If work on the critical path stays on schedule, the entire project stays on schedule. Non-critical work can be delayed without impacting the overall timeline.

The network graph cannot be complete while forgotten or unnecessary work items exist. A complete graph shows the current state of the entire plan.

Before beginning production, verify that all stakeholders have considered every interface point with other stakeholders.

Surprises appear at the moment of connection. What was not thought of in advance appears at the moment of handoff.

Cell (metaphor for a basic unit or module):

For large teams, maintain a matrix mapping each colleague to every other colleague.
Cell (metaphor for basic unit or module): Each cell indicates whether one party needs something from the other.

This interdependence matrix becomes a source of confidence that all potential intersections are covered and will not become surprises or blockers later.

Most startups and inexperienced performers fail at the acceptance-handoff procedure.

A responsible party produces the result. The consuming party receives it. A record of this exchange must exist.

The result specification is mandatory. Without a specification, there is no proof the work happened.

The result specification includes a commissioning program. The commissioning program is the scenario that proves the result works.

Together, the result specification and commissioning program answer two questions: what components are present, and how do we verify each one functions.

When a result does not satisfy the consumer, the discussion concerns the specific characteristics, not the result in general. The consumer can raise the question of advisability.

Without these documents, the handoff becomes a source of problems where people feel compelled to assert themselves.
With these documents, only substantive corrections appear, and they enter as new orders in a new release cycle.

Construction begins only after the commissioning scenario for the result exists.

The commissioning scenario describes the first action, the first system response, and the first achieved outcome.

Writing the commissioning scenario first reveals whether the creator understands the core sequence of operations. Vague commissioning reveals vague thinking.

The commissioning scenario can be discussed with the collective and with clients before the procedure begins. Raising it early increases precision and completeness.

Format for commissioning: if someone takes the result and runs the commissioning scenario, do this action, or that action?

Subject actions before building. Then build only what the commissioning scenario reveals.

A manager is anyone who distributes resources to accomplish a goal through others.

If you lead a complex project requiring planning and visualization of a future state, you are a manager.

Even a team of one involves three people: your past self, your present self, and your future self. Coordination between these versions requires management.

Anyone involved in creating software, launching products, starting a business, or engaging in entrepreneurship is a manager.

The key requirement of the manager: distinguish important from unimportant, maintain focus on the important while holding the complete picture, stay adequate to reality,
and
sustain this for projects lasting one to five years.

From the over-system, a request arrives. From that request, the manager composes goals for which team resources will be expended to produce the requested result.

The final products of work are information and physical materials. Information includes documents, media products, and program components. Physical materials are produced according to defined documents.

Only subject actions matter. To extract meaning from information flows, practice written thinking. Write an essay on the topic. Only through writing do reasoning connections and conclusions form.

The output is a bank of ideas from which candidate projects are drawn to direct activity.

All decisions divide into two groups: justified decisions and intuitive decisions.

Justified decisions use scientific methods. Their outcomes can be predicted from known data and patterns.

Intuitive decisions are made when: the patterns governing the process are unknown, the speed of the process leaves no time for justification, or the decision maker lacks necessary knowledge.

Each decision is characterized by: the goal to be achieved, the time
and
place of execution, the resources required, the conditions of the process, the organization of management,
and
the composition of interacting processes.

For a justified decision, each parameter must have a quantitative measure. Without this, patterns cannot be identified or used.

Theory is the observable explanation of the portion of the world in which your work operates.

Theory serves three purposes. It enables confident production of what is needed. It allows consistent communication with stakeholders. It provides the foundation for verification and support.

Theory protects projects from scope expansion through new and useful ideas. Ideas are always more numerous than available capacity. Theory filters out guaranteed non-purposeful work.

Theory enables rapid onboarding. Good theory explains the essence of the work quickly. Bad theory leaves questions.

Theory must exist as a written document. Without written theory, knowledge travels from head to head through broken telephone, losing pieces along the way.

Theory is not ontology, thesaurus, or reference manual. Theory is a coherent narrative explaining the key questions of the work.

Productivity is the ratio of obtained result to labor expended. This formula governs all human activity from primitive tools to complex systems.

The basic requirement for living organisms: obtain twice as many calories as are spent. The basic metabolic cost plus the cost of obtaining calories from the environment.

Primitive tools reduce energy expenditure without changing the amount of result. This ratio is recorded as the artifact, the tool.

The fundamental human need is the improvement of the tool.
This act of creation transforms both nature and the person themselves.
This act of creation is the act of creating the future.

All levels of Maslow's pyramid are achieved through action. There is no distinction between the forms of labor or acts of creation at any level.

People unite for collective work based on the objective necessity of producing the means of life.

The lifetime and labor of one person is limited. One person cannot satisfy all their needs alone, even with full expenditure of physical resources.

Progress has produced goods and services so complex that one person cannot produce them from scratch.
Try to make a computer mouse without purchasing raw materials.
Behind the mouse is an entire production chain from mineral extraction onward.

Collective labor follows the laws of system development. The law of wholeness and emergence states that when elements are combined, their capabilities multiply, not add.

This multiplication of capability through collective organization is why humans continue advancing.

A stated collective goal may not match the leader's private goal. The degree of divergence between stated and private goals is the measure of personal morality.

If hidden goals do not prevent achievement of collective goals, the degree of morality is acceptable for the position.

On the distance, divergence between stated and private goals of managers will manifest in contradictory actions and will inevitably destroy the managed object.

The more complex the managed object, the higher the required morality of the manager. And vice versa.

The manager needs the complete list of all work of all subordinates, ordered and calibrated.

Compiling this list is the manager's primary duty.

The signal that enters the system must be a document. The document governs what happens. Without the document, the signal disperses.

Every organizational unit must produce documents. The quality signals are: observability without special tools, personalization by name, and historical tracking.

These three signals form the foundation for all management information.

The plan for obtaining results is a quasi-test of professional fitness of the assembled workers.

If a person cannot formulate a plan for how they see obtaining the result, the question arises: how will they obtain it if they do not understand what must be done?

Many workers are deeply uninterested in providing such a plan because it removes their ability to vary by situation
and
at the last moment say: here is what I wanted to do for you.

With such people, large projects cannot be planned, and the manager's work comes into question.

Planning must happen before resources are spent. Understanding possible problems and avoiding unnecessary expenses and unnecessary results is the purpose.

Planning takes time. The principle: the correctly posed question is half the answer.

The plan emerges iteratively. The control questions for the plan must be asked regularly: who does what when where and how much.

This produces iterative plan development.

A common mistake: infinite agile. Planning is iterative but has a definitive end. Infinite planning without deadlines is either laziness or corruption.

Projects without deadlines and accountability are the result of people consciously introducing the fog of uncertainty for unlimited budgets.

The traditional approach treats the worker as capable of creativity and independent judgment.
The commander sends soldiers to take an objective.
During execution, the commander is not in direct contact.
Full creative initiative happens locally.
The only outcome structure is: objective reached or not reached.
No discussion of bushes, ravines, or weather conditions.

The modern approach treats the worker as incapable of correct decisions, requiring strict adherence to instructions.

The gap between these approaches explains the paradox of IT specialists who produce creative work but resist creative responsibility.

Implement delocracy. Structure management so that the results completed by workers speak for themselves, indicating how much and how well each employee advanced the work or lacks results.

The reward system must not be given to subordinates to manage. If it becomes the object of their work, the result is inverted.

Resource distribution and management is work. If you give resource orders to executors, your resources run out quickly.

Distribute rewards and recognition in traditional mode, but tie the grounds for rewards to specific accomplishments.

All materials and technical means reduce to one function: transfer of something from somewhere to somewhere, in space or time.

The movement of objects in space defines logistics. Movement through time defines storage.

Any transport function requires expenditure of some resource: free energy flow or power expenditure.

Creative rivers flow where delocracy operates. Bureaucracy blocks creative rivers.

YouTube and RuTube both launched with resources and patronage. One has rivers of creative flow. The other does not.

Bureaucracy produces compliance. Delocracy releases creative flow.

The delocracy framework maps directly to how regenerative communities govern their work.

Goal: a water system that produces consumed water for members.

Result: the installed water system. Engaged and used by members.

Task: assigned to a member who specifies their result list and dependencies.

Theory: written description of the water system, why it is needed, how it works, how to verify it works.

Commissioning scenario: the sequence of actions that proves the system delivers water.

Interdependence matrix: who needs what from whom for the water system to function.

Network graph: dependencies between all results. Critical path identified.

Specification and handoff: documented proof of what was built and how to verify it.

Engagement rate: how many times members actually use the water system.

This is the complete loop from abstract goal to consumed result in community context.
