# First Principles Framework Fpf Rigorous Epistemic Thinking

The First Principles Framework is a meta-theory of rigorous thinking by Anatoly Levenchuk.

It provides a technical specification for reasoning, not a management methodology.

The purpose is to make thinking inspectable, auditable, and precise enough that agreements are binding.

Every concept is described by its first principles. No proprietary labels.

The foundational unit is the holon: an entity that is simultaneously a whole and a part of something larger.

Every holon maintains its identity as a whole while being a component of a larger whole.

This captures the fundamental duality: individual autonomy within collective membership.

A person is a whole agent and a part of a community. A community is a whole and a part of a region.

The holonic kernel means that every entity in the framework has both whole and part properties.

The framework defines precise role types.

U.RoleAssignment is a contextual role occupied by a specific agent in a specific context.

U.Capability describes what an agent can do based on demonstrated competence.

U.PromiseContent is the content of a commitment made by a role occupant.

U.EvidenceRole is the accountability role that witnesses and records commitments.

U.RoleStateGraph is a graph of named states that a role occupant moves through.

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):

U.RoleAlgebra describes the formal relations between roles in a system.

U.Commitment is a deontic statement that creates binding obligation.

U.SpeechAct is a communicative action that creates effects in the social world.

U.Method is a transformer that acts on an input to produce an output. It has a describedEntity that specifies what it acts upon.

U.MethodDescription specifies the epistemic constraints on a method. It describes what counts as valid description, valid execution, and valid evidence of success.

Methods are not just procedures. They include the epistemic framework for knowing when the method has been applied correctly.

The framework distinguishes between two temporal perspectives.

Open-ended evolution describes the unfolding of a system over time without predetermined endpoint.

Closed-world completion describes the state when a system has reached its designed endpoint.

Most real systems operate in open-ended evolution. Projects are designed to reach closed-world completion, but the surrounding context continues open-ended evolution.

The signature stack provides the discipline of precise declaration.

U.Signature is a law-governed declaration made by an agent about a subject on a bounded context. It creates a commitment.

U.Mechanism is the application of a law to produce an effect. Signatures create mechanisms that constrain future behavior.

U.EffectFreeEpistemicMorphing describes how knowledge can change without affecting the commitments already in place.

U.EpistemicViewing describes how agents observe and interpret evidence.

U.EpistemicRetargeting describes how the focus of inquiry shifts when new evidence requires different questions.

Precision restoration suites provide a methodology for upgrading fuzzy language to precise form.

Boundary discipline is the practice of making explicit statements about what is inside and what is outside a system.

A boundary statement declares what a system includes and excludes. It specifies the semantic frame for all statements within it.

Without boundary discipline, concepts leak. Terms mean different things to different people. Agreements fail because the scope was never clarified.

BoundedContext defines the scope within which a particular vocabulary and set of commitments apply.

Ontological parsimony requires that entities be multiplied only when necessary.

If two phenomena can be explained with the same underlying entity, do not introduce a new entity.

This keeps models lean. Complexity should come from interaction of simple components, not from proliferation of entity types.

The framework recognizes that complex systems require multiple perspectives simultaneously.

An episteme is a slot graph that captures a particular way of knowing about a system.

Engineering views are projections from epistemes that highlight specific aspects.

MVPK (Multi-View Publication Knowledge) defines typed surfaces for publishing these views.

No single view captures everything. Multiple views must be maintained in alignment.

Four characteristics define the quality of a reasoning architecture.

Auditability means reasoning can be inspected and verified by others.

Evolvability means the architecture can change without breaking its core commitments.

Composability means components can be combined to form new wholes.

Falsifiability means claims are structured to be testable and potentially wrong.

The framework rests on nine foundational commitments.

These include the holonic kernel, explicit creativity and assurance, bounded contexts, multi-view architecture, epistemic hygiene, and others.

These commitments define the identity of the framework. Changing them produces a different framework.

When language is fuzzy, the framework provides a workflow to achieve precision.

Step 1: detect umbrella words that cover multiple meanings under one term.

Step 2: unpack the local ontology by identifying all distinct meanings the umbrella word covers.

Step 3: choose a mathematical substrate that can represent the distinct meanings as distinct entities.

Step 4: refactor the model using the mathematical substrate.

Step 5: mint precise lexemes with clear definitions and guardrails for use.

The Tech/Plain twin approach creates a technical term for precision use and a plain term for everyday communication.

The Evidence Graph tracks all commitments, evidence of fulfillment, and relationships between them.

Each node is a commitment made by a role occupant.

Edges connect commitments to evidence that supports or contradicts fulfillment.

The graph enables accountability: anyone can trace whether commitments were honored.

A regenerative community uses FPF for precision in governance agreements.

Why-Cards become U.Signatures with law-governed declarations about community commitments.

Time-Locks become U.Commitments with epistemic constraints on when actions can proceed.

Barn Raising becomes U.EvidenceRole assignments where witnesses record community contributions.

The evidence graph becomes the community memory of what was agreed and what was fulfilled.
