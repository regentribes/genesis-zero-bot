# Blockchain And Ethereum Fundamentals

Blockchain is essentially a shared digital ledger that lives across many computers instead of in one central place.

Every time someone makes a transaction, it gets grouped with other transactions into a block.
Each block gets chained to the previous one in a way that makes it nearly impossible to alter past records.

The magic that keeps everyone honest is consensus. The network agrees on which transactions are real before adding them to the chain.

Mining is the process where computers compete to solve complex puzzles to validate blocks and earn rewards.
This is how the system secures itself without needing a bank or any central authority.

Ethereum took blockchain beyond simple money transfers by introducing smart contracts.
Smart contracts are programs that automatically execute when conditions are met, like a vending machine that dispenses something once you insert the right amount.

The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) is the worldwide computer that runs these contracts.
Everything that happens on Ethereum costs a small fee called gas.
This prevents people from spamming the network and also pays the people who keep the system running.

Tokens are another big idea on Ethereum.
They represent everything from ownership stakes to digital art to voting rights.
All living on the same infrastructure.
This is why people call it blockchain 2.0.

The people who love Ethereum tend to see it as more than a technology.
They see it as a movement toward a fairer world where anyone can participate in finance without needing a bank.

They believe in DeFi (decentralized finance).
The idea that you can lend, borrow, trade, and earn interest directly with other people through code instead of through institutions that take a cut.

Many Ethereum supporters own tokens in projects they believe in.
Not just to make money but because they want to support a more open and transparent financial system that no single company or government controls.

Regular people outside the crypto world often see the whole thing as a giant gamble or a bubble waiting to pop.
Like the famous tulip mania in Holland centuries ago where people lost fortunes on flower bulbs.

They find it hard to understand why sending a few dollars should cost twenty dollars in fees.
Or why something called gas has nothing to do with filling up a car.

To many, blockchain seems overly complicated for problems that simpler solutions could solve.

Blockchain represents one approach to trust without institutions. The principles of distributed consensus and transparent ledgering are relevant to community exchange systems.

The limitation: current blockchain implementations are energy-intensive and speculative.regen communities need low-energy consensus mechanisms that serve practical exchange rather than financial gambling.
