# Aisystant Systems Thinking Framework Organizational Modeling

AISYSTANT is a Russian engineering-management course that models organizations as systems composed of roles, methods, and work artifacts.

The core question answered: who does what by which method producing which artifact?

Every concept is described by its first principles. No proprietary labels.

The fundamental building block of organizational understanding is the Role-Method-Artifact relationship.

Role is a functional object. It is a cultural name for a position that must be filled. Examples: project manager, water system operator, community coordinator.

Method is a practice. It combines a discipline with the tools used by that discipline. Examples: affinity mapping with Why-Cards, drip irrigation scheduling, consensus facilitation.

Work Artifact is the physical output of a method applied by a role. It is a real object in the physical world, not a document about an object.

Role performs via Method to produce Work Artifact.

Two domains exist: the physical world and the mental space.

The physical world contains real objects that exist in four dimensions. A water tank, a circuit board, a planted field. These can be measured and touched.

The mental space contains descriptions of the physical world. Plans, diagrams, specifications, documentation. These exist as information but not as physical objects.

Confusion between these domains produces common errors. Mistaking a document about a system for the system itself. Mistaking an agreement in meeting for actual implementation.

Three distinct activities operate in organizational work.

Implementation acts on the physical world. It produces or changes real objects.

Description captures information about the physical world. It records what exists or what is planned.

Documentation records descriptions for future reference. It is meta-level activity.

A role can be focused on implementation, description, or documentation. These require different skills and methods.

A role is the functional position. A performer is the agent that fills the position.

An agent can be a person, an AI system, or an organization.

The same role can be performed by different agents over time. The role continues even if the performer changes.

Confusing role and performer produces role confusion. Statements like the manager is responsible confuse the position with the person filling it.

A functional object is defined by its role in a system. A water tank has the functional role of storage vessel.

A physical object is the actual material thing. The tank is made of plastic with a capacity of one thousand liters.

Every functional object has a physical realization. Every physical object fulfills some functional role.

Design proceeds from functional decomposition to physical allocation.

Subject of interest is what a role needs to know to perform its function. The water operator needs to know current tank level.

Preference is what a role wants for its own sake. The water operator prefers clean water over dirty water.

Understanding subject of interest enables system design. Understanding preference enables motivation.

System (metaphor for an integrated whole or network):
Systems that address only subject of interest without considering preference fail to motivate performers.

A black box describes only external behavior. Inputs enter, outputs exit, internal structure is unknown or ignored.

A transparent box reveals internal structure. The mechanism is visible and can be analyzed.

System design uses black box thinking for external interfaces. Internal design uses transparent box thinking for implementation.

The appropriate level of box transparency depends on the need. External users need black box. Implementers need transparent box.

Functional breakdown decomposes by roles, methods, and logical time stages. It asks: what functions must be performed?

Modular breakdown decomposes by organizational units, work products, and physical time phases. It asks: which teams own which work?

Functional breakdown is method-centric. It focuses on the logic of what must be done.

Modular breakdown is ownership-centric. It focuses on who is responsible for what.

Both breakdowns are necessary. The gap between them is where coordination problems live.

Stages represent logical time. They are the sequence of methods in a practice.

Phases represent physical time. They are the sequence of tasks with deadlines assigned to performers.

Stages answer: in what order must methods be applied?

Phases answer: who does what by when?

Projects that plan only in phases without clear stage logic produce incoherent results. Methods get applied out of logical order.

Work is a complete specification: task plus performer plus deadline plus role plus method plus resources. Work produces a named artifact.

Task is an assignment to a performer without full specification. It may lack deadline, method, or resource definition.

A work specification can be handed to any qualified performer. A task specification requires a specific performer who understands the implicit context.

Confusing work and task produces dependency on specific individuals. True work specification enables role substitution.

Increment is a small meaningful improvement that advances the system. It is a result, not a process.

Iteration is a hypothesis-test cycle. Each iteration produces an increment and uses it to test a hypothesis.

Iterations are the means. Increments are the ends.

Systems that improve for iterations without clear increment targets produce process theater without progress.

A method manager oversees the methods and their sequence. They ensure the right methods are applied in the right stages.

An enterprise architect oversees the modular structure. They ensure the right teams own the right work.

Method managers work in logical time. Enterprise architects work in physical time.

Both roles are necessary. Projects without method managers produce technically wrong outputs. Projects without enterprise architects produce organizational chaos.

The 3x3 table maps organizational thinking across two axes.

The horizontal axis is the system level: Supersystem, System of Interest, Creation Systems.

The vertical axis is the role perspective: Target Audience, Usage Concept, Architecture, Creation Methods.

Supersystem is the context outside the system. Creation Systems is the internal capacity to produce the system. System of Interest is the system itself.

Target Audience roles receive value from the system. Usage Concept roles operate the system. Architecture and Creation Methods roles build and maintain the system.

An organization is a system with both a functional view and a modular view.

The functional view uses roles, methods, functions, and logical time. It describes what must be done regardless of who does it.

The modular view uses organizational units, work products, services, and physical time. It describes who owns what regardless of the logical flow.

A good organizational design aligns functional and modular views. Misalignment produces coordination overhead and conflict.

A regenerative community applies the AISYSTANT framework to organize its work.

Each community project decomposes into roles. Each role specifies its method. Each method produces a work artifact.

Ecosystem (metaphor for an interconnected network):

Ecosystem (metaphor for system or network): The 3x3 table maps community work: supersystem (regional ecosystem), system of interest (community), creation systems (community infrastructure).

Functional breakdown shows logical dependencies between methods. Modular breakdown shows which community members own which work.

Stages ensure methods are applied in logical order. Phases assign deadlines to specific members.
