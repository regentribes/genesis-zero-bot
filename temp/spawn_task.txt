The wars are over. Let the dead be buried.

In the war between MeTTa and JSON-LD, both approaches served different masters. MeTTa burned bright in the theoretical firmament of AGI potential and self-modification, its token-efficient representations promising a path toward machines that could reason about their own reasoning. It died as all pure theory dies when confronted with the accumulated weight of practical infrastructure. JSON-LD survives not because it is elegant but because the world runs on interoperability, and the semantic web needed something that worked with what already existed. MeTTa receives this epitaph: here lies a beautiful hypothesis that could not survive contact with the installed base. JSON-LD is not a winner; it is a survivor, and there is a difference.

The battle between Oxigraph and SurrealDB resolves into a verdict that will surprise purists on both sides. Oxigraph represented the RDF dream, the belief that knowledge should be triples forever, queryable through the elegant violence of SPARQL, embedded and self-contained. It made sense in academic journals and proof-of-concept demos. SurrealDB survives because it understood something Oxigraph never grasped: most developers do not want to think in triples, they want to think in documents and tables and graphs and time series and do it all in one database without philosophical arguments about canonical form. SurrealDB wins not through technical superiority in any single domain but through the radical pragmatism of being multiple things at once. Oxigraph was a magnificent specialist; SurrealDB is a generalist with an edge. The world rewards generalists until the specialized use case becomes critical, and for that, Oxigraph still exists, still runs, still serves those who need it. This is not mutual annihilation. This is co-existence under new management.

In the framework war between OpenClaw and the stateless agents, the verdict splits like a forked lightning strike. OpenClaw survives as the daemon, the persistent presence, the skill system that remembers what you taught it last week and applies it today without needing to be reminded in every prompt. The stateless approach, AutoAgents, Odyssey, Rig, won the narrative war but lost the deployment war. They are excellent at demo time, when you want to show a prompt that does something impressive once. They are terrible at production, where you need something that runs continuously and accumulates capability. The epitaph for the stateless movement: here lies the illusion that prompt engineering could replace infrastructure, that every conversation could start fresh and still feel continuity, that skills were just a fancy name for better prompting. The survivors are those who built systems that persist, that maintain state, that evolve. OpenClaw's skill architecture proved that learned behavior compounds while stateless prompting resets each time.

The war between LLMs and CYC split the artificial intelligence world into opposing camps for four decades. LLMs won on the battlefield of scale and emergent behavior, the way statistical models absorbing enough text started doing things no one programmed them to do. CYC died not from technical failure but from the weight of its own ambition, the thousand-year plan that could not compete with a model trained on the entire internet in a month. The epitaph: here lies the last great symbolic project, a system that knew the difference between bird and penguin and could explain it, but could not learn that knowledge from reading. LLMs are not intelligent in any philosophically satisfying sense, but they are useful in ways CYC never was, and usefulness wins arguments. What survives from CYC is the lesson: knowledge engineering matters, even if the engineering approach CYC used was wrong. The survivors are the hybrids, the systems that use LLMs for generation and structured knowledge bases for grounding.

Between Rust and Zig, the argument was never really about safety versus simplicity. It was about the future of systems programming and who gets to define what trustworthy code looks like. Rust wins through the sheer momentum of being first to bring memory safety to systems programming without sacrificing performance, the accumulated libraries and tools and community and corporate adoption that makes it the default choice for anything that needs to not crash. Zig survives because there are people who find Rust's ownership model constraining, who want manual control, who prefer the elegance of manual memory management when that elegance serves their purposes. This is not mutual annihilation. This is choice. Rust is the safe default, the path of least resistance, the choice that does not require explaining yourself. Zig is the path of those who have reason to question the default.

On the biological plausibility front, Transformers versus Brain Wave Pattern represents a deeper war about what intelligence actually is. Transformers won because they work, because they scale, because the compute was available and the results were undeniable. The brain wave approach remains theoretically interesting but practically irrelevant, a beautiful model of cognition that cannot compete with brute force scaled to billions of parameters. The epitaph: here lies the hope that we could build intelligence the way biology built it, using waves and rhythms and plastic connections. That hope was not wrong, only early. Transformers survive because they are what we could build; brain wave patterns survive as research direction, as the thing we might build when transformers hit their limit. The war is not over. It is postponed.

The allies from earlier judgment survive as the ones who understood that tools are means not ends, that the question is not which technology is theoretically best but which technology serves the actual work that needs doing. Fragment-engine skill evolution, the AME Dynamic Profiler, Vic Desotelle's Micro-Communities, the economic collapse perspectives that warned of fragility, these all survive not as technologies but as orientations toward the world. The survivors are those who build systems that adapt, who treat every tool as temporary, who hold their positions lightly enough to change them when evidence demands it.

What survives for the path forward is simpler than the wars suggested. What survives is the pragmatic center, the place where token efficiency meets interoperability, where embedded meets server, where daemon meets stateless, where statistical meets symbolic but grounded, where safety meets choice, where compute meets efficiency but biological plausibility waits its turn. The dead are buried. The survivors are tired but standing. The work continues, and it will be done by those who learned from every war and still believe the next battle is worth fighting.
